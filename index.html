LONELINESS

I recently (as in two months ago) finished reading “Together” by Vivek Murthy, the former U.S. Surgeon General. It’s a book about loneliness, and I really recommend it — super insightful! I thought I'd note down here some lessons that I learned. At this point, I’m not sure if I remember everything anymore, but I still think some points have been molded into my daily life.

In not as eloquent words as Murthy, I shall start with a more obvious one: the antidote to loneliness is paradoxically being alone. But being alone is not loneliness, it’s solitude. To me, solitude is intentional, while loneliness is a feeling we oftentimes may find ourselves in unintentionally (I hope so, at least). I often find that especially with technology and the Internet omnipresent in our lives, it is difficult to take moments to truly just think and just be. For example, walking or thinking for two or three minutes of silence does wonders for my clarity of mind, but I rarely do set aside time to do so. It’s easier to grab my phone or laptop and sink into their abyss. (also side note: Tori Kelly’s new album, aptly named “Solitude,” is really good and I recommend listening)

I learned that solitude isn’t the only antidote, however. I can talk about these for ages and ages, so will not resort to rambling here for the moment.

What I thought was most mind blowing, and what I’m trying to work off of, is how to bridge the gap between people different from us. Evolution-wise, I think it makes a lot of sense that we tend to gravitate toward people more like us — potentially dangerous “outsiders” could potentially steal your food or kill you. And realistically, it’s easier to relate to someone more like you. But, I think there’s a huge value in talking to people way different from us, whether that be politically, racially, in socioeconomic status, etc. Murthy focused on the example of political differences, outlining how today’s society has been increasingly polarized. However, in the midst of all this, the son of a white nationalist/KKK president somehow has reversed course of all his views simply by becoming friends with a non-white friend who he loved to play music with. They never even talked about politics/his white nationalist views, but his views gradually changed simply by seeing the humanity in his friend. Obviously I don’t think this could always work to convert some Trump toady to a Bernie backer, per say, but I really resonated with that — at the end of the day, we are all human and can find at least some commonalities in the human experience. Those should be focused on, he said, not the differences.

Ultimately, the book prompted me to think of how I could better form and foster more meaningful relationships. After all, relationships with people around us, meaningful relationships, are those that keep us going and prevent us from fully experiencing the epidemic that is perpetual loneliness. That, along with my experiences the past year working on a depression module, leads me to emotions.

EMOTIONS

This may also seem obvious, but I have come to the conclusion that emotions are very important to forming deeper and more meaningful connections with people. Why is it that when we talk about our struggles, our happiest moments, our feelings, it is often when we are most vulnerable, yet also most intimate with others? Even with someone who could not be more different from us, why is it that our shared emotional feelings may be an important thread linking us together as humans?

Nevertheless, emotions are difficult, period. As a more logically inclined person, I think they are even more difficult. I think it is especially because truly listening for emotions and what someone has to say is essential to tuning into other’s emotions and finding your own. I am a pretty bad listener. Over the years, as I learned to tune out constant parental nagging, I may also have tuned out actually important conversations. I would like to improve, so let me know if you want to talk about it! Also feel free to disagree, would like to hear other thoughts!

CODING

I used to swear against learning how to code. And now I am here… What changed? Especially as coronavirus hit, I began realizing the increasingly growing need and utility of knowing how to code. After taking a few coding courses, I’ve been a bit inspired by the potential of everything that you can do! But it does take a lot of time. My fear is that like many other hobbies in my life, coding will lose its novelty and soon become too frustrating and boring for my liking. Nevertheless, I think it’s a cool skill to have, especially in this day and age, and I’m finding that I’m willingly devoting more time to coding instead of other commitments like classes. For website development, I recommend Khan Academy and Codecademy! However, if you know HTML, CSS, or Javascript, I need help!
